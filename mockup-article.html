The Tyranny of the Marginal User

Why I Rescued This

This essay crystallizes something I've felt for years but couldn't articulate. The constant dumbing down of tools in the name of "accessibility" is actually making them less accessible to those who want to go deep. A must-read for anyone who cares about the future of software.

When a product gets too popular, the marginal user becomes the king. The marginal user is someone who just started using your productâ€”someone who doesn't understand it, doesn't care about it, and will probably leave soon. But product metrics are dominated by them.

Consider Instagram. When it started, it was a place for photographers to share their work. The community valued craft, composition, and aesthetic excellence. Early users spent time carefully editing their photos, writing thoughtful captions, and engaging in meaningful conversations about photography.

But as Instagram grew, the marginal user changed everything. They didn't care about photographyâ€”they just wanted to share selfies and see what their friends were doing. The product evolved to serve them: filters became simpler, the explore page prioritized viral content over quality, and the entire platform shifted from a photography community to a social network.

The Metrics Problem

This happens because of how we measure success. Product teams track engagement, retention, and growth. When you're small, power users dominate these metrics. But as you scale, the marginal userâ€”by sheer volumeâ€”drowns out the signal from your core users.

"The tragedy of scale is that optimizing for the median means abandoning the edges where all the interesting stuff happens."

Every A/B test is dominated by marginal users. Every feature decision is justified by how it affects their behavior. And slowly, inexorably, the product becomes something the original users no longer recognize.

Products don't die from lack of features. They die from loss of soul.

The Power User Exodus

Eventually, power users leave. They leave because the product no longer serves them. They leave because the community has changed. They leave because the values that drew them in have been optimized away in pursuit of growth.

And here's the cruel irony: losing power users doesn't show up in your metrics right away. They're a small percentage of your user base. The marginal users keep coming, engagement stays high, and everything looks fine. Until it doesn't.

What Can Be Done?

The solution isn't to stop growing or to ignore new users. It's to build products with depthâ€”products that have a high ceiling for mastery while maintaining a gentle learning curve. It's to resist the temptation to optimize every surface for the marginal user.

Some products get this right. Figma is complex enough for professionals but accessible enough for beginners. Notion has layers of depth that reward exploration. These products understand that serving power users and marginal users isn't mutually exclusiveâ€”it's about designing for growth rather than dumbing down for scale.

The best products don't just solve problems. They create possibilities. And possibilities require depth, complexity, and room for mastery. Don't let the marginal user kill that.
